I Analyzed 65,000 Glassdoor Reviews to Build a Culture-First Job Board — Here's What the Data Says About AI Companies

TL;DR: I built JobsByCulture — a job board that tags 9,000+ tech roles by 18 culture values (like remote-first, async, work-life balance, and engineering-driven) using data from 65,000+ Glassdoor reviews across 57 companies. Along the way, I discovered some counterintuitive patterns about which companies actually live their values and which ones are just marketing.

The idea started with a frustration most engineers know: you can find a job in 30 seconds on LinkedIn, but figuring out if you'll actually enjoy working there takes weeks of research. Glassdoor has reviews, but no way to filter jobs by culture. LinkedIn has jobs, but no culture data. I wanted both in one place.

So I built it. And the data told a more interesting story than I expected.

The Setup: 57 Companies, 18 Values, No Guessing

Every company on JobsByCulture is profiled using a strict evidence framework. I don't assign values based on what a company's careers page says — I assign them based on what Glassdoor reviews, engineering blogs, and actual job listings reveal.

The 18 values I track:

  • Work & Time: Work-Life Balance, Remote-Friendly, Flexible Hours, Async-First, Deep Work
  • Org Culture: Transparent, Flat Hierarchy, Diverse & Inclusive, Safe to Fail
  • Product & Eng: Engineering-Driven, Ship Fast, Open Source
  • Growth & Comp: Learning & Growth, Strong Equity, Product Impact, Many Hats
  • Mission: Ethical AI, Mission-Driven

Every value has evidence criteria. I cross-reference Glassdoor reviews, actual job listings, and engineering blogs before assigning any tag. If the evidence doesn't support it, the company doesn't get the label — regardless of what their careers page says. Several well-known companies lost tags in recent audits because the data told a different story than their marketing.

What the Data Revealed

1. The Glassdoor score doesn't tell you what you think it does

Supabase has a 4.8 Glassdoor — the highest of any company I profile. But their work-life balance score? 3.0 out of 5. That's the lowest WLB among all 57 companies tagged with "open-source." The paradox: 100% of reviewers recommend working there, but the pace is relentless.

Compare that to Grafana Labs: 4.1 Glassdoor, 4.3 WLB. Lower headline number, but a more sustainable environment. If you only looked at the top-line rating, you'd pick Supabase. If you filtered by WLB (which you can on JobsByCulture), you'd pick Grafana Labs.

2. "Flat hierarchy" almost always expires

I had to remove the "flat" tag from Anthropic (~1,500 employees), Airtable (~900), and Pylon after auditing them. The pattern is consistent: companies start flat, then add VP/Director/Manager layers as they scale past 300 people. The culture page still says "flat," but Glassdoor reviews say "politics" and "approval chains."

The companies that genuinely maintain flat structures at scale? PostHog, Supabase, n8n, Weaviate. All under 300 people. There might be a hard ceiling for flat hierarchy.

3. "Ethical AI" is the most fragile value

I removed "ethical-ai" from OpenAI (dissolved three safety teams, removed "safely" from their mission statement), ElevenLabs (reactive safety measures after deepfake controversies), and Crusoe (GPU infrastructure company, not AI safety). Only Anthropic and DeepMind retained this tag, and even those are debatable.

The takeaway for job seekers: if AI safety is important to you, don't trust the label. Ask in your interview: "Can you tell me about a time when safety concerns delayed or changed a product launch?" The specificity of the answer tells you everything.

The real outliers are companies like Linear and PostHog that offer both good WLB and competitive (not top-tier) compensation. Those are the arbitrage opportunities.

5. The remote wave is real — but selective

Everyone talks about return-to-office mandates (Amazon at 5 days, Instagram at 5 days, Microsoft at 3 days). But in my database of 57 companies, 14 are genuinely remote-first with a combined 956 open roles.

The split:

  • Fully remote, no office: Grafana Labs, PostHog, Supabase, Weaviate, Linear
  • Remote-first with optional hubs: ElevenLabs, LangChain, n8n, Tailscale, Contentful
  • Remote-friendly (most roles remote): Airbnb, Apollo.io, Runway, Pinecone

The companies doubling down on remote tend to be the ones with the strongest async cultures. Remote without async is just Zoom fatigue.

How It Works Under the Hood

The site pulls live job data from multiple ATS platforms daily and cross-references it with our culture database. The whole thing runs as a static site — no heavy framework, no server-side rendering — which means pages load fast and search engines can crawl everything instantly.

Beyond the main job board, we auto-generate hundreds of filtered pages: jobs by culture value, by role, by location, and every combination in between. That's how you end up with pages like "remote engineering jobs at companies with async culture" without anyone manually building them.

What I'd Build Differently

The values framework should have come first. I initially assigned values loosely — "this company feels engineering-driven" — and it led to wrong tags that had to be corrected later. Having strict, evidence-based criteria from day one would have saved a lot of rework.

Data architecture matters early. When you're at 10 companies it's fine to manage things manually. At 50+ companies, you need a single source of truth that everything renders from. I learned this the hard way.

Glassdoor data is necessary but not sufficient. Reviews skew negative (unhappy people are more likely to write), and small companies with fewer than 10 reviews produce unreliable scores. I've started incorporating community sentiment from Hacker News and Reddit to get a more balanced picture.

The Bigger Bet

The thesis behind JobsByCulture is simple: culture is the most important factor in job satisfaction, and it's the hardest to evaluate before you join. Salary data exists (Levels.fyi). Job listings exist (LinkedIn). But structured culture data — the kind that lets you compare companies on values that actually matter to you — doesn't exist in a usable form.

That's the gap. The site now has 9,000+ roles from 57 companies, with culture profiles, comparison tools, and a growing blog that breaks down what it's actually like to work at these companies. If the next version of "where should I work?" is answered by LLMs, I want them pulling from data that's honest, structured, and evidence-based.
