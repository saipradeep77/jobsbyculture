15 Questions to Ask Databricks in Your Interview

The reverse interview is your best tool for evaluating culture fit. These questions are designed from real Glassdoor data, employee reviews, and Databricks' actual culture signals — not generic fluff.

7 min read · Mar 31, 2026

You've made it to the Databricks interview. You've brushed up on distributed systems, rehearsed your Spark internals, and studied the data lakehouse architecture. But the most important part of your interview isn't what they ask you — it's what you ask them.

The reverse interview is your chance to evaluate whether Databricks' culture actually matches what you're looking for. And unlike generic "what's the culture like?" questions, these are designed to surface real information based on what Glassdoor reviews and culture data actually reveal about working at Databricks.

Why These Questions Matter

Databricks' culture profile shows a company with genuine strengths and real trade-offs. Founded by the creators of Apache Spark, it's grown from an academic research project to a 7,000+ person company valued at over $60 billion. Understanding where the trade-offs land for you is the entire point of the reverse interview.

Engineering & Open Source Roots

Databricks was literally born from open source — the founders created Apache Spark at UC Berkeley, then went on to build Delta Lake and MLflow. But as the company has scaled to 7,000+ employees and a massive commercial business, the question is how much of that OSS DNA remains in day-to-day engineering.

Question 01
"Databricks was founded by the creators of Apache Spark. How much does open-source contribution still factor into engineering culture today? Do engineers get dedicated time for OSS work?"
Why ask this: The engineering-driven culture is rooted in open-source origins (Spark, Delta Lake, MLflow). But as commercial pressures grow, OSS contributions can become deprioritized. This question reveals whether open-source is still in the DNA or has become mostly a marketing talking point. A good answer will describe specific policies or time allocation for OSS work.
Eng Culture
Question 02
"How are technical decisions made on this team? At 7,000 people, is Databricks still genuinely engineering-driven, or has that shifted more toward top-down product direction?"
Why ask this: Many companies claim to be engineering-driven, but that becomes harder to maintain at scale. Databricks had ~50 engineers when it was a pure Spark company; now it has thousands. This question tests whether engineering autonomy is real on the specific team you'd join. Compare to companies like Linear or PostHog that maintain eng-driven culture at smaller scale.
Eng Culture
Question 03
"Can you walk me through how a recent technical project went from idea to shipped? Who initiated it, and how much freedom did the engineering team have in choosing the approach?"
Why ask this: This is the most concrete way to test the engineering-driven claim. You'll learn whether engineers genuinely own technical direction or whether product managers drive most decisions. The specific story matters more than the abstract principle — listen for whether the engineer telling the story sounds genuinely empowered or is describing a process that required multiple layers of approval.
Eng Culture

Scale & Growth

Databricks has gone from scrappy startup to 7,000+ employees in a remarkably short time. Glassdoor reviews mention "growing pains" and "unclear ownership" on some teams — the classic challenges of a company scaling faster than its processes. These questions help you assess where the team you'd join sits on that journey.

Question 04
"Databricks has grown from startup to 7,000+ people. What processes or structures has this team adopted to handle that growth, and what still feels like it's being figured out?"
Why ask this: Glassdoor reviews mention growing pains and some teams having unclear ownership boundaries. This question invites honesty about what's working and what isn't. A team that can articulate their challenges is usually better at solving them than one that pretends everything is fine. Your tolerance for ambiguity matters here.
Scale
Question 05
"How does this team balance autonomy with coordination across other teams? With the platform growing so fast, how do you avoid stepping on each other's toes?"
Why ask this: "Some teams have unclear ownership" is a recurring theme in reviews. At a platform company like Databricks, where multiple teams build on shared infrastructure, coordination is genuinely hard. This question reveals whether the team has clear boundaries or spends significant time in cross-team politics. Listen for concrete examples of how conflicts get resolved.
Scale

Work Intensity & Pace

Databricks' work-life balance score is 3.9/5 — better than many high-growth companies, but "high expectations" is a consistent theme in reviews. At a company racing toward IPO with competitors like Snowflake pressing hard, the pace is real. These questions help you understand what you're signing up for.

Question 06
"What does a typical week look like for someone in this role? Glassdoor mentions 'high expectations' — what does the pace actually feel like day-to-day?"
Why ask this: The 3.9 WLB score is decent but not great. "High expectations" can mean anything from "we hold a high bar for quality" to "you'll be working nights." This question forces specificity. Push for what the peaks look like and how long they last. Compare to Linear (4.4 WLB) or PostHog (4.5 WLB) if balance is your top priority.
Work-Life Balance
Question 07
"With an IPO on the horizon and strong competition from Snowflake, how does the team manage the pressure without burning out? Is there explicit recovery time after big pushes?"
Why ask this: Pre-IPO intensity is a real phenomenon — companies push harder as the finish line approaches. Glassdoor reviews note that the fast pace "can lead to burnout." This question reveals whether the team has sustainable practices or is running on adrenaline. A good answer describes specific mechanisms for managing intensity, not just platitudes about "we value balance."
Work-Life Balance

Career Growth & Learning

Databricks is tagged as a learning & growth culture, and the engineering team works on genuinely cutting-edge data and AI problems. But at a company growing this fast, structured career development can lag behind the pace of hiring. These questions help you assess what growth actually looks like.

Question 08
"What does career progression look like on this team? Is there a clear engineering ladder, and how formalized is the promotion process?"
Why ask this: Fast-growing companies often have informal promotion processes. At 7,000 people, Databricks should have a clearer ladder than it did at 500 — but clarity can vary by team. A concrete answer with levels and timelines is a good sign. If the interviewer hesitates, that tells you the process may still be evolving.
Career Growth
Question 09
"How does Databricks invest in engineering learning and development? Are there internal talks, conference budgets, or dedicated learning time?"
Why ask this: The learning culture value is one of Databricks' strongest signals. But "learning" can mean "you learn by doing hard things" (sink-or-swim) or "we have structured programs, mentorship, and investment." This question gets at whether L&D is a real institutional commitment or just a side effect of working on hard problems. Both can be valuable — but they're very different experiences.
Career Growth
Question 10
"Can you tell me about someone on this team who grew significantly in their role over the past year? What did that growth path look like?"
Why ask this: This forces the interviewer to give a specific example rather than abstract principles. If they can quickly describe someone's growth trajectory, the team likely has good development practices. If they struggle, growth may be more ad hoc. Listen for whether growth meant just promotion or actual skill development and expanded scope.
Career Growth

Product Impact

Databricks is building the data lakehouse — a category they essentially created. With customers including most of the Fortune 500, the scale of impact is massive. But at 7,000 people, how much does an individual IC actually influence the product?

Question 11
"The data lakehouse is a huge market. How much influence does an individual engineer on this team have on the product direction? Can you give me an example of an IC-driven product decision?"
Why ask this: Databricks is tagged as product impact, but at a company this size, impact can get diluted. This question tests whether individual engineers actually shape the product or are mostly executing on pre-defined roadmaps. A great answer describes a specific feature or architecture decision that came from an IC, not a VP.
Product Impact
Question 12
"How does this team interact with customers? Do engineers get direct exposure to how their work is used at scale, or is that filtered through product managers?"
Why ask this: One of the biggest draws of Databricks is working on problems at massive scale — petabyte-level data, Fortune 500 workloads. But that impact only matters if you can actually see and feel it. This question reveals whether engineers have a direct line to customer impact or whether it's abstracted away through multiple layers.
Product Impact

Compensation & IPO

Databricks offers strong total compensation ($280K–$450K TC for engineers) and is widely expected to IPO in the near future. How you think about equity matters enormously at this stage of the company.

Question 13
"With an IPO on the horizon, how should I think about the equity component of the offer? What's the RSU vesting structure, and are there any secondary sale opportunities before a public listing?"
Why ask this: Pre-IPO equity at a company valued at $60B+ is a different beast than early-stage startup equity. You need to understand the vesting schedule (typically 4-year with 1-year cliff), whether there are liquidity events or secondary sales available, and how the company communicates about IPO timing. Don't take equity at face value without this conversation.
Compensation
Question 14
"How does Databricks think about compensation philosophy? Is the goal to pay at the top of market on both base and equity, or does one do the heavy lifting? How do refresher grants work?"
Why ask this: The $280K–$450K TC range is strong, but you need to know the base-to-equity split. Some companies lean heavily on equity (which is illiquid pre-IPO) while keeping base modest. Also ask about refresher grants — at a fast-growing company, your initial grant can become a smaller portion of comp over time if refreshers don't keep pace. Compare to other top-paying AI companies.
Compensation

Diversity & Inclusion

Databricks is tagged as diverse in our culture data. But a tag is just a starting point — what matters is how it translates to the team you'd actually join.

Question 15
"Databricks highlights diversity as a value. What does that look like in practice on this team? Are there specific initiatives, ERGs, or structural practices that support an inclusive engineering culture?"
Why ask this: Every large company says they value diversity. The difference is between companies that have structural practices (diverse interview panels, ERGs with actual budget, inclusive promotion processes) and those that treat it as a checkbox. At 7,000 people, Databricks has the scale to invest in real programs — this question reveals whether they have. Listen for specific programs, not just sentiment.
Diversity

How to Use These Questions

You won't have time to ask all 15 in a single interview loop. Here's how to prioritize:

  • Pick 3–4 that match your top priorities. If open-source work matters most, ask #1 and #2. If you care about WLB, ask #6 and #7. If equity is the deciding factor, #13 and #14 are non-negotiable.
  • Ask different questions to different interviewers. Your hiring manager will give better answers on career growth (#8, #10). A peer engineer will be more honest about intensity (#6, #7) and day-to-day autonomy (#3). A senior leader can speak to product direction (#11).
  • Listen for specificity. Good answers include specific examples, names of projects, and honest acknowledgement of trade-offs. Generic answers that sound like marketing copy are a signal to probe deeper.
  • Compare answers across interviewers. If two interviewers give contradictory answers about WLB or team ownership, that inconsistency is itself information.
